My PhD research has aimed at gaining insights into how fetal brain development occurs, using image processing and analysis, computational modeling, and deep learning techniques. I have been a member of the MULTI-FACT EraNeuron Project, which aims to provide the scientific community with an open-access multi-centric pipeline for fetal processing data (current version: fetpype).

I first defined a complete 3D computational pipeline to build and validate patient-specific mechanical models of brain development. Starting from the processing of fetal brain magnetic resonance images (MRI), personalized finite-element 3D meshes were generated, in which biomechanical models were run to simulate brain development. Several metrics were then employed to compare simulation results with neonatal images from the same subjects, on a common reference space. We applied the computational pipeline to a cohort of 29 subjects where fetal and neonatal MRI were available, including controls and ventriculomegaly cases.

I participated in two FeTA Challenges hosted within the MICCAI conference. During the first challenge, the goal was to create a deep learning algorithm capable of accurately segmenting tissues within the fetal brain using data from a single center. This involved considering various factors such as different gestational ages (GA), conditions (normal/abnormal), various superresolution reconstruction algorithms, and MRI quality. In the second challenge, the objective shifted to developing domain generalization algorithms. These algorithms aimed to create robust solutions capable of addressing the domain gap observed when using multicentric data. Participants were provided with cases from two distinct centers, and their algorithms were rigorously tested against datasets from these centers as well as from two additional centers not included during training.

In the last stage of my PhD, I did a research stay at the Auckland Bioengineering Institute, to collaborate with Gonzalo Maso-Talou to accelerate FEM simulations using a deep-learning-based surrogate model.
